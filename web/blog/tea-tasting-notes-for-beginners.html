Tea Tasting Notes for Beginners: How to Describe What You're Drinking

March 16, 2026 · 10 min read

You take a sip of tea. It tastes… good. Maybe really good. But when someone asks you to describe it, your brain produces exactly one word: "tea-like."

You're not alone. Most people drink tea for years without ever learning to articulate what they're tasting. Wine drinkers get sommelier culture, coffee drinkers get cupping notes on every bag. Tea drinkers? We mostly just say "smooth" and hope that covers it.

Here's the thing: you already have the palate. You just don't have the vocabulary yet. Tea tasting notes for beginners aren't about developing a superhuman tongue — they're about connecting the flavors you already notice to words you can use.

What are tasting notes, exactly?

Tasting notes are descriptors that capture what a tea smells like, tastes like, and feels like in your mouth. They're not scientific measurements. They're comparisons — bridges between what's in your cup and things you already know.

When someone says a Darjeeling tastes "muscatel," they mean it reminds them of muscat grapes. When they describe a Japanese sencha as "marine" or "umami," they're reaching for the closest flavor analogy their brain can find.

That's all tasting notes are: honest analogies. There's no wrong answer.

The five things to pay attention to

Professional tea tasters evaluate a lot of dimensions, but you only need five to start. Think of them as layers, from first impression to lingering memory.

1. Aroma (before you sip)

Bring the cup to your nose before anything else. The dry leaves have one aroma; the brewed liquor has another; the wet leaves at the bottom have a third. Each one tells you something different.

Common aroma categories: floral, fruity, vegetal, nutty, earthy, smoky, sweet, spicy, marine. Don't force it — just ask yourself: "What does this remind me of?" If the answer is "roasted almonds," write that down.

2. Flavor (what you taste)

The big one. Take a sip and let it sit on your tongue for a second before swallowing. Your tongue detects five basic tastes — sweet, salty, sour, bitter, and umami — but flavor is much richer than that. Flavor is taste plus aroma combined.

Ask yourself:

  • Is it sweet? What kind of sweet — honey, caramel, brown sugar, fruit?
  • Is there bitterness? Gentle and pleasant (like dark chocolate) or sharp and astringent?
  • Do you taste anything green or vegetal? Fresh grass, steamed spinach, seaweed?
  • Is there fruit? Citrus, stone fruit, berries, tropical?
  • Anything roasted, toasty, or nutty?

3. Body (how it feels)

Body is about weight and texture, not flavor. Think of the difference between skim milk and whole milk — same general taste, completely different mouthfeel.

Tea body ranges from thin and watery to thick and syrupy. A light-bodied white tea feels delicate and airy. A full-bodied aged pu-erh coats your mouth like broth. Neither is better — they're just different.

Words for body: thin, light, medium, full, thick, silky, creamy, brisk, smooth, velvety.

4. Finish (what lingers)

After you swallow, pay attention. Does the flavor vanish immediately, or does it stick around? A long finish — where flavors keep evolving for 10, 20, even 30 seconds — is usually a sign of quality. Some teas leave a sweet aftertaste ("huigan" in Chinese tea vocabulary). Others leave a pleasant cooling sensation in your throat.

A short finish isn't necessarily bad. Some teas are meant to be clean and crisp. But noticing the finish helps you understand why some teas feel more complex than others.

5. Evolution (how it changes)

This one's a bonus, but it's where things get interesting. Good tea changes. The first sip might be floral. By the third cup, it's nutty. Steep it a few more times and something honeyed emerges.

This is especially true with gongfu brewing, where you steep the same leaves multiple times in quick succession. Each steep reveals a different face of the tea. Tracking that evolution is one of the most rewarding parts of tea tasting.

A flavor vocabulary cheat sheet

Stuck on words? Here are common tea tasting notes organized by category. You don't need to memorize these — just scan the list next time you're sipping and see what resonates.

Floral

Jasmine, orchid, rose, honeysuckle, lilac, chrysanthemum, gardenia, violet. Common in: oolongs, white teas, green teas, scented teas.

Fruity

Citrus (lemon, bergamot, grapefruit), stone fruit (peach, apricot), berry (blackberry, raspberry), tropical (lychee, mango, passion fruit), dried fruit (raisin, fig, date). Common in: Darjeelings, fruity oolongs, some black teas.

Vegetal & Herbal

Fresh grass, steamed greens, spinach, seaweed, hay, mint, eucalyptus, artichoke. Common in: Japanese greens, some Chinese greens, herbal infusions.

Nutty & Toasty

Roasted almond, chestnut, walnut, toasted grain, popcorn, bread crust, brown rice. Common in: hojicha, genmaicha, some oolongs, roasted teas.

Sweet

Honey, caramel, brown sugar, molasses, vanilla, maple, cotton candy, malt. Common in: golden tips, aged whites, some Assams, dessert-like teas.

Earthy & Woody

Wet earth, forest floor, mushroom, leather, cedar, sandalwood, tobacco, camphor. Common in: pu-erh, aged oolongs, some black teas.

Mineral

Slate, granite, wet stone, chalk, iron. Common in: Wuyi rock oolongs (yancha), some Darjeelings, terroir-driven teas.

Spicy

Cinnamon, clove, ginger, black pepper, cardamom, anise. Common in: masala chai blends, some Ceylons, aged pu-erh.

How to practice (the three-cup method)

Here's a simple exercise that will develop your palate faster than anything else:

  1. Brew three different teas side by side. Pick teas that are obviously different — say, a green tea, a black tea, and an oolong. Same water, same steep time.
  2. Taste them in order, back and forth. Sip the green, then the black, then the oolong. Go back to the green. The contrast makes differences obvious that you'd never notice in isolation.
  3. Write down three words for each tea. Just three. Force yourself to be specific. Not "good" — what kind of good? Not "smooth" — smooth like silk or smooth like butter?

Do this once a week for a month and you'll be shocked how much your vocabulary expands. You're not learning new tastes — you're learning to notice what was always there.

Common mistakes beginners make

Overthinking it

If you taste apple, say apple. Don't second-guess yourself because you think apples aren't "supposed" to show up in a Taiwanese oolong. Tasting is subjective. Your palate, your notes.

Only focusing on flavor

Body, finish, and aroma are just as important. Two teas can have similar flavors but feel completely different in your mouth. A thin, crisp sencha and a thick, buttery gyokuro might both taste "vegetal," but the experience is worlds apart.

Not writing it down

This might be the biggest one. You think you'll remember. You won't. Even a few words scribbled in your phone are better than nothing. Better yet, keep a tea journal — it's the single best tool for building your palate over time.

Comparing yourself to experts

Professional tea tasters have been doing this for decades. They can detect notes you've never heard of. That's fine. You're not trying to pass an exam. You're trying to enjoy your tea more deeply and remember what you loved.

Tasting notes by tea type

Different tea types tend to fall into predictable flavor families. Here's a rough map to orient yourself:

  • Green tea: Vegetal, grassy, marine, sweet, nutty. Japanese greens lean umami and marine; Chinese greens lean nutty and sweet.
  • White tea: Delicate, floral, honey, hay, melon. Subtle — you have to pay attention.
  • Oolong: The wildcard. Light oolongs are floral and creamy. Dark oolongs are roasted, fruity, mineral. Oolongs vs greens are a study in contrast.
  • Black tea: Malty, sweet, brisk, tannic, fruity, spicy. Depends hugely on origin — Assam tastes nothing like Darjeeling.
  • Pu-erh: Earthy, woody, mushroom, leather, sweet. Aged pu-erh develops incredible depth over decades.

These are starting points, not rules. You'll find green teas that taste like flowers and oolongs that taste like chocolate. That's what makes tea interesting.

Why bother with tasting notes?

Fair question. You can enjoy tea without ever describing it. But learning to articulate what you taste does three practical things:

  1. Better buying decisions. When a vendor describes a tea as "honey, stone fruit, light body," you'll know whether that sounds like your thing. No more guessing.
  2. Better brewing. If your tea tastes bitter, you'll recognize it as astringency and know to lower the temperature or shorten the steep. Tasting vocabulary helps you diagnose and fix problems.
  3. More enjoyment. Attention amplifies pleasure. The more you notice in your cup, the more interesting every sip becomes. A tea that seemed "fine" becomes fascinating once you start picking apart its layers.

Start simple, stay curious

You don't need to become a certified tea taster. You just need to slow down, pay attention, and write down what you notice. Three words per cup. That's it.

Over time, your vocabulary will grow naturally. You'll start noticing the mineral backbone in a rock oolong, the vegetal snap of a first-flush Darjeeling, the way a good matcha finishes sweet long after you've swallowed it.

And the next time someone asks what your tea tastes like, you'll have a much better answer than "good."
