Chai Tea Recipe: How to Make Real Chai at Home

March 4, 2026 · 10 min read

Let's get one thing out of the way: "chai tea" is redundant. Chai means tea. Saying "chai tea" is like saying "tea tea." But language evolves, and if you searched "chai tea recipe," you're probably looking for the spiced, milky, soul-warming drink that India runs on — not a linguistics debate.

So here's what most Western cafés get wrong: they start with a syrup or a powder. Real chai starts with whole spices, crushed and simmered with strong black tea and milk. The difference isn't subtle. It's the difference between a photograph of a sunset and actually standing outside watching one.

This guide covers the classic masala chai recipe, explains why each ingredient matters, and gives you enough variations to keep your mornings interesting for months. No special equipment required. Just a saucepan and a few minutes.

What makes chai, chai?

At its core, chai is just black tea simmered with spices and milk. That's it. But within that simple formula, there's enormous variation — every household in India has their own version, and asking which is "correct" is a good way to start an argument at a family dinner.

The constants across most recipes:

  • Strong black tea — usually CTC Assam (crush-tear-curl), which brews dark and malty fast. Loose leaf Assam works too.
  • Whole spices — at minimum, cardamom and ginger. Everything else is regional preference.
  • Milk — simmered in, not added after. This is the key technique that separates chai from "tea with milk."
  • Sugar — optional but traditional. Chai is meant to be a bit sweet.

The method matters as much as the ingredients. Chai isn't steeped like English tea — it's cooked. The spices and tea simmer together, extracting flavor aggressively. If your chai is delicate and subtle, something went wrong.

The classic masala chai recipe

Masala Chai — Serves 2

Ingredients:

  • 1½ cups water
  • 1 cup whole milk (or oat milk — it holds up best)
  • 2 tablespoons loose black tea (CTC Assam or strong Assam)
  • 4 green cardamom pods, lightly crushed
  • 1-inch piece fresh ginger, sliced or grated
  • 4 whole black peppercorns
  • 1 small cinnamon stick (cassia or Ceylon)
  • 2 whole cloves
  • Sugar or jaggery to taste (start with 2 teaspoons)

Method:

  1. Crush the spices. Give the cardamom pods a light crack with the flat of a knife. No need to remove the seeds — the pods add flavor too. Same with the peppercorns.
  2. Simmer the spices. Add water and all the spices to a small saucepan. Bring to a boil, then reduce to a simmer for 3–4 minutes. You want the kitchen to smell like your best decision today.
  3. Add the tea. Add the loose tea leaves and simmer for another 2 minutes. The liquid should turn deeply dark.
  4. Add the milk. Pour in the milk and bring back to a simmer. Watch it — milk likes to boil over when you look away. Let it simmer for 2–3 minutes until the color is a rich, warm brown.
  5. Sweeten and strain. Add sugar or jaggery, stir to dissolve. Strain into cups through a fine mesh strainer.

Total time: ~10 minutes

A few notes on technique:

  • Don't skip the simmer. If you just steep the spices like a tea bag, you'll get a fraction of the flavor. The boiling extracts essential oils that hot water alone won't touch.
  • Grate the ginger for more punch. Sliced ginger gives a gentler warmth. Grated ginger makes it assertive. Both are right — it depends on your mood.
  • The boil-over trick: When the milk threatens to rise, blow gently across the surface or lift the pot off the heat for a second. Experienced chai-makers let it rise and fall 2–3 times for better integration.

Choosing the right tea

This is where most recipes under-deliver. They say "black tea" and move on. But the tea matters enormously.

CTC Assam (the traditional choice)

CTC stands for crush-tear-curl — a processing method that produces small, granular pellets. CTC teas brew fast, dark, and strong. They're designed to stand up to milk and sugar without disappearing. If you want chai that tastes like what you'd get from a roadside chaiwala in Mumbai, this is it.

Most Indian grocery stores carry CTC Assam (brands like Red Label, Wagh Bakri, or Tata Gold). It's inexpensive and purpose-built for chai.

Loose leaf Assam

Higher-quality whole-leaf Assam (like a second flush from Mangalam or Halmari estate) will give your chai more complexity — malty, slightly honeyed notes underneath the spice. It won't brew as dark or as quickly as CTC, so increase the simmer time by a minute or two.

What doesn't work

Earl Grey, Darjeeling, and most flavored blacks are too delicate for chai. They'll get bulldozed by the spices. Green tea and white tea aren't the right base either — chai needs the tannic structure of a full-bodied black tea to balance the milk and sugar. The spice pantry: what each spice does

You can make great chai with just cardamom and ginger. Everything else is enhancement. Here's what each spice contributes, so you can build your own blend intentionally:

  • Cardamom — The essential chai spice. Floral, slightly eucalyptus-like, with a cooling menthol finish. Green cardamom is standard; black cardamom (smoky, camphor notes) is used in some regional variants.
  • Ginger — Warmth and bite. Fresh is better than dried for chai. Increases the "warming" sensation, especially good in cold weather.
  • Cinnamon — Sweet warmth without sugar. Cassia (the common grocery store kind) is more assertive. Ceylon cinnamon is more delicate and floral. Either works.
  • Black pepper — Subtle heat that lingers in the back of the throat. Also traditionally believed to aid digestion and bioavailability of other compounds.
  • Cloves — Intense, numbing warmth. Use sparingly — one too many and cloves will hijack the whole cup. Two cloves per two cups is plenty.
  • Star anise — Licorice sweetness. Not in every masala chai, but common in Kashmiri and some South Indian styles. One star per pot.
  • Fennel seeds — Gentle anise flavor, common in Gujarati chai. Slightly cooling effect that balances the other warming spices.
  • Nutmeg — Just a tiny grating. Warm, woody, slightly sweet. Goes a long way.

Variations worth trying

Adrak chai (ginger-forward)

Double or triple the ginger. Skip the cinnamon and cloves. This is the version you make when you feel a cold coming on, when it's January, or when you just want something that hits harder. Grate the ginger instead of slicing it for maximum effect.

Kashmiri kahwa

A green tea–based chai from Kashmir that breaks all the rules above. Use green tea (yes, really), saffron, cardamom, cinnamon, and crushed almonds. No milk. Sweetened with honey. It's lighter, more aromatic, and completely different from masala chai — but equally warming.

Cutting chai (Mumbai street-style)

"Cutting" means half a cup — the standard serving at chai stalls. The technique is what matters: pour the finished chai between two vessels from a height to create froth and aerate it. This changes the texture noticeably. Strong, sweet, tiny cup — it's designed to be consumed in three sips while standing on a sidewalk.

Iced masala chai

Make the recipe above but use less water (1 cup instead of 1½). Sweeten a bit more than usual — cold dulls sweetness. Let it cool slightly, then pour over a full glass of ice. The thermal shock creates a beautiful swirl. For more cold tea techniques, see our cold brew tea guide.

Chai concentrate (batch prep)

Make the spice-and-tea base at 2x concentration (double the spices and tea, same water). Strain and refrigerate. When you want chai, heat equal parts concentrate and milk. Keeps for 5 days. This is the lazy-but-smart approach.

Making your own masala blend

Once you've made chai a few times, consider making a jar of your own spice blend. It cuts the prep time to about 30 seconds per cup.

Base masala blend (makes ~20 cups):

  • 20 green cardamom pods
  • 10 whole cloves
  • 2 cinnamon sticks
  • 1 tablespoon black peppercorns
  • 1 teaspoon fennel seeds (optional)
  • ½ a nutmeg

Toast the spices in a dry pan over medium heat for 2–3 minutes until fragrant (not smoking). Let them cool, then grind to a coarse powder. Store in an airtight jar. Use 1 heaping teaspoon per two cups of chai. Add fresh ginger separately — dried ginger works in the blend but isn't the same.

The milk question

Whole milk is traditional and gives the richest body. But this is your chai — use what you like:

  • Whole milk — Rich, creamy, the gold standard.
  • Oat milk — The best dairy alternative for chai. Creamy, slightly sweet, simmers well without splitting.
  • 2% milk — Fine. Slightly thinner but still good.
  • Coconut milk — Makes a wonderful variation. Tropical undertones that play well with cardamom.
  • Almond milk — Can split when boiled. Add it later and keep the heat gentle.

Whatever you use, the key technique is simmering the milk with the tea and spices, not adding it at the end. This is what creates the integrated, velvety texture of real chai. If you're coming from a coffee background, think of it less like adding cream and more like the milk in a hot chocolate — it's cooked in, not poured on.

Common mistakes

  • Using pre-ground spices from a jar. Ground spices lose their volatile oils within weeks. Whole spices, freshly crushed, are non-negotiable for good chai. It's the same principle as gongfu brewing — freshness of your starting material determines the ceiling of your cup.
  • Not enough tea. Chai should be strong. The milk and sugar will dilute it. If you can see the bottom of the cup, add more tea next time.
  • Steeping instead of simmering. Chai is cooked, not steeped. The spices need the aggressive heat to release their oils fully.
  • Skipping the sugar. You don't have to make it sweet, but a small amount of sugar or jaggery rounds out the spices and enhances the milk. Try it both ways and decide.
  • Too many cloves. This is the most common overdose. Cloves are potent. Two per pot. That's it.

Why chai is more than a recipe

In India, chai isn't really a "recipe" you follow — it's a daily ritual shared with family, neighbors, coworkers, and strangers. Chai stalls are the original coworking spaces. Business deals happen over chai. Arguments are settled over chai. Bad news is delivered over chai, because somehow the act of holding a warm cup makes hard things slightly more bearable.

When you make chai at home, you're tapping into something that billions of people have been doing for centuries. Not because it's trendy or photogenic, but because it works. The spices wake you up. The milk grounds you. The warmth settles you. And the ritual of making it — crushing, simmering, waiting — is its own form of calm in a cup.
