How Much Caffeine in Tea? The Complete Chart by Type

March 18, 2026 · 11 min read

"How much caffeine is in tea?" is one of those questions that sounds simple until you try to answer it. The real answer is: it depends on the tea, how you brew it, and how much leaf you use. But that's not very helpful when you're standing in the tea aisle wondering if this cup will keep you up at 11 PM.

So here's the practical version. This guide covers caffeine content for every major tea type — with actual milligram ranges per cup, not vague labels like "low" and "high." We'll also cover why the ranges vary so much, what factors you can control, and how tea caffeine compares to coffee, espresso, and other drinks.

The caffeine in tea chart

All values are for a standard 8 oz (240 ml) cup, brewed at the recommended temperature and time for each tea type. These are ranges, not absolutes — we'll explain why below.

Tea Type Caffeine (mg per 8 oz) Relative Level
Matcha 60–70 mg
Black tea (Assam, CTC) 50–90 mg
Black tea (Darjeeling, Yunnan) 40–70 mg
Pu-erh (shou/ripe) 30–70 mg
Oolong tea 30–50 mg
Green tea (Chinese) 20–45 mg
Green tea (Japanese) 25–50 mg
Gyokuro 30–50 mg
White tea 15–30 mg
Herbal "tea" 0 mg

For comparison:

  • Drip coffee: 95–200 mg per 8 oz
  • Espresso: 63 mg per 1 oz shot
  • Cola: 22–46 mg per 12 oz
  • Energy drinks: 70–200+ mg per serving

A few things jump out. First, even the strongest tea (CTC Assam) maxes out below the low end of drip coffee. Second, the ranges overlap significantly — a strong green tea can have as much caffeine as a mild black tea. Third, herbal teas have zero caffeine because they aren't actually tea. They're infusions of other plants. If the distinction matters to you, we covered it in depth in our herbal tea vs real tea guide.

Why the ranges are so wide

You'll notice most tea types have a 20-40 mg range. That's not sloppy data — it reflects real variability. Here's what drives it:

1. Leaf age and part of the plant

Young buds and tips contain the most caffeine — it's a natural insecticide the plant concentrates in its newest, most vulnerable growth. A tea made from buds (like Silver Needle white tea or golden-tip Assam) will have more caffeine per gram of leaf than a tea made from mature leaves.

This is why the common belief that "white tea is low caffeine" is misleading. Silver Needle is all buds — it can have more caffeine per gram than some black teas. But it's brewed at lower temperatures and shorter times, which extracts less of it. We got into the details in our white tea benefits guide.

2. Water temperature

Hotter water extracts more caffeine. This is the biggest single variable you control. Black tea brewed at 212°F (100°C) releases its caffeine much faster than green tea brewed at 175°F (80°C). Our steeping temperature guide has the full breakdown by tea type.

3. Steep time

Longer steep = more caffeine. A 1-minute steep of green tea might extract 15 mg; the same tea at 5 minutes could hit 40 mg. This is also why gongfu brewing — multiple short steeps — gives you more control over your caffeine intake per cup, even though you use more leaf overall.

4. Leaf-to-water ratio

More leaf per cup means more caffeine per cup. A Western-style brew (2-3 grams per 8 oz) will have less caffeine than a gongfu session (5-7 grams per 4 oz). But in gongfu, each steep is short and you drink it across many small pours — so the experience is more gradual.

5. Processing and oxidation

Oxidation itself doesn't significantly increase or decrease caffeine. A green tea and a black tea from the same leaf will have similar caffeine per gram. The difference in your cup comes primarily from brewing parameters, not from how the leaf was processed. This surprises a lot of people.

What does matter: CTC (crush-tear-curl) processing — used for most tea bags — breaks the leaf into small particles that release caffeine faster. A tea bag of CTC Assam will extract caffeine much more aggressively than whole-leaf Assam at the same temperature and time. If you're weighing loose leaf vs tea bags, caffeine extraction speed is one of the real differences.

Caffeine by tea type: the details

Black tea: 40–90 mg

Black tea has the broadest range because the category is enormous. A delicate first-flush Darjeeling and a malty CTC Assam are both "black tea," but they behave differently in the cup.

Higher caffeine: CTC Assam, breakfast blends (English Breakfast, Irish Breakfast), Kenyan black teas. These use broken or CTC-processed leaves and are designed for strong extraction — often with milk.

Lower caffeine: Whole-leaf Chinese blacks (Yunnan Gold, Keemun, Lapsang Souchong), Darjeeling second flush. The whole leaf releases caffeine more slowly, and these teas often taste best at slightly lower temperatures.

If you're switching from coffee, black tea is the natural starting point. It won't match coffee milligram for milligram, but paired with L-theanine it delivers energy without the jitters. We covered our full tea vs coffee comparison separately.

Green tea: 20–50 mg

Japanese green teas tend to run slightly higher than Chinese greens because of how they're processed. Japanese greens are steamed (which preserves more of the leaf's compounds), while Chinese greens are pan-fired (which applies more heat and can reduce some volatile compounds).

Within Japanese greens, there's significant variation. Sencha sits in the middle of the range. Bancha and hojicha (made from mature leaves or roasted) run lower. Gyokuro and kabusecha (shade-grown) run higher because shade-growing increases both caffeine and L-theanine. We covered all the Japanese green tea types in detail.

The single most important thing you can do for green tea caffeine control is temperature. Green tea should never touch boiling water — 160-180°F (70-82°C) is the window. Higher extracts more caffeine and more bitterness. Lower keeps it smooth and gentle. Our green tea brewing guide walks through exact temps and times.

Matcha: 60–70 mg

Matcha breaks the rules because you consume the entire ground leaf, not just a steep. You're ingesting all the caffeine in that leaf, not the fraction that hot water extracted. This makes matcha the highest-caffeine tea — comparable to a weak cup of coffee.

But matcha is also the highest-L-theanine tea, by a wide margin. The caffeine experience is radically different from coffee or even from brewed black tea. It's sustained, focused, and smooth. We broke down the full comparison in matcha vs green tea.

Ceremonial-grade matcha (stone-ground from shaded tencha) will have more L-theanine than culinary grade, which matters for the quality of the energy — not the quantity of caffeine.

Oolong tea: 30–50 mg

Oolong is the broadest category by processing — anywhere from 10% to 85% oxidized. Lighter oolongs (like Taiwanese high-mountain) trend toward the lower end; darker oolongs (like Da Hong Pao or Wuyi rock teas) trend higher.

The gongfu factor matters a lot here. Oolong is the tea most commonly brewed gongfu-style — lots of leaf, tiny pot, quick steeps, many rounds. Your first steep might pull 15-20 mg. By steep five, extraction drops. Over a full session you might consume 60-80 mg total, but spread across 45 minutes. It's caffeine microdosing by design. For the full oolong vs green tea comparison, we have a dedicated piece.

White tea: 15–30 mg

White tea is minimal-processing tea — the leaves are withered and dried with very little intervention. The caffeine content per gram of leaf can actually be quite high (especially Silver Needle, which is pure buds), but white tea is typically brewed at low temperatures (175-185°F) for shorter times, which limits extraction.

If you want the lightest caffeine hit, Shou Mei or Bai Mu Dan (White Peony) are your best bets — they use a mix of buds and leaves, and the mature leaves bring less caffeine. Silver Needle is buds-only and runs hotter. More on white tea in our white tea benefits guide.

Pu-erh: 30–70 mg

Pu-erh is the wild card. Shou (ripe) pu-erh, which has been accelerated through fermentation, tends to sit in the 30-50 mg range. Sheng (raw) pu-erh, especially young sheng from old-growth trees, can push higher — 50-70 mg isn't unusual.

Pu-erh also responds dramatically to brewing method. The standard gongfu approach — rinse, then many short steeps — gives you the smoothest caffeine curve. Grandpa-style (leaf sitting in hot water all day) will extract everything. We covered brewing and styles in the pu-erh tea guide.

Herbal infusions: 0 mg

Chamomile, peppermint, rooibos, hibiscus, lavender — none of these are from Camellia sinensis, so none contain caffeine. (One exception: yerba maté, which is its own plant with its own caffeine — roughly 30-50 mg per cup — but it's not commonly grouped with herbal teas.)

If you're looking for an evening drink with zero caffeine, herbals are your answer. We covered the best options for winding down in our best tea for sleep guide, and the calming options in our tea for anxiety piece.

How to control caffeine in your cup

You can't change the tea plant. But you can change how you brew, and that's where most of the variation comes from.

To reduce caffeine:

  • Lower your water temperature. Drop from 212°F to 185°F and you'll cut caffeine extraction by 20-30%.
  • Shorten your steep. A 2-minute steep extracts roughly half the caffeine of a 5-minute steep.
  • Use less leaf. 2 grams per cup instead of 3 grams makes a real difference.
  • Choose later steeps. In gongfu brewing, the first steep extracts the most caffeine. By steep 3-4, caffeine drops significantly.
  • Try cold brewing. Cold water extracts 40-60% less caffeine than hot water. Plus the flavor is smoother and sweeter.

To increase caffeine:

  • Use boiling water. For teas that can handle it (black, dark oolong, pu-erh).
  • Steep longer. 5+ minutes will extract most of the available caffeine.
  • Use more leaf. Simple math — more leaf, more caffeine.
  • Choose bud-heavy teas. Silver Needle, golden-tip Assam, gyokuro.
  • Drink matcha. You're consuming the whole leaf. Maximum extraction by default.

The myth of "rinsing out" caffeine

You've probably heard this: "Steep your tea for 30 seconds, dump that water, then steep again — you've removed most of the caffeine." It's a persistent myth, but the research doesn't support it.

A 30-second rinse removes only about 5-10% of the total caffeine in the leaf. Caffeine extraction is progressive and roughly linear for the first several minutes. To remove a meaningful amount, you'd need to steep for 3-5 minutes — and at that point, you've also extracted most of the flavor compounds you actually wanted.

If you need low caffeine, choose a tea that's naturally lower or adjust your brewing parameters. Don't rely on the rinse trick.

Caffeine sensitivity: finding your range

Caffeine metabolism is largely genetic. Some people clear caffeine in 3-4 hours; others take 8-10. If you're sensitive, here's a practical approach:

  1. Start with white tea or light green tea. 15-30 mg is enough to notice without overwhelming.
  2. Track your response — Resteeped's tea journal lets you log how each tea makes you feel, not just what it tastes like.
  3. Set a caffeine curfew. If you're caffeine-sensitive, switch to herbal by 2 PM. If you metabolize quickly, 4-5 PM might be fine.
  4. Remember L-theanine. Tea caffeine feels different from coffee caffeine because of the L-theanine buffer. Even if you're sensitive to coffee, you might tolerate tea surprisingly well. We covered the full mechanism in tea for energy without jitters.

The bottom line

Tea caffeine is real, but it's also manageable. Most teas deliver 20-70 mg per cup — roughly a quarter to half of a cup of coffee. The variables you control (temperature, time, leaf amount, brew method) matter as much as the tea type itself.

The best approach isn't to obsess over milligrams. It's to understand the general range of what you're drinking, pay attention to how it makes you feel, and adjust from there. Tea gives you that flexibility — which is one of the reasons it's a better daily caffeine vehicle than coffee for most people.
