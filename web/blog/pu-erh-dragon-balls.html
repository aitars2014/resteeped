Pu-erh Dragon Balls: What They Are, How to Brew Them, and How to Roll Your Own

March 16, 2026 · 10 min read

Somewhere between a pu-erh cake and a loose-leaf session, there's a format that solves one of pu-erh's biggest friction points: the pry. If you've ever wrestled a pu-erh pick into a compressed cake at 7 AM before coffee, you know the feeling. Dragon balls skip all of that. Each one is a single serving of hand-rolled pu-erh, ready to drop into a gaiwan and go.

The Chinese name is longzhu (龙珠), literally "dragon pearl." They're typically 5 to 8 grams of pu-erh leaves rolled into a tight sphere, wrapped in cloth and dried until they hold their shape. One ball, one session. No pu-erh pick required, no guessing the weight, no accidentally breaking off too much cake and ending up with tea that could strip paint.

They've been gaining traction with both casual drinkers and the gongfu crowd, and recently a viral Reddit post showing the hand-rolling process at home got the community talking. Turns out you can make them yourself with some loose pu-erh, steam, and a bit of patience.

Here's everything you need to know: what dragon balls are, why they're worth trying, how to brew them properly, and how to roll your own.

What are pu-erh dragon balls?

Dragon balls are a relatively modern format for pu-erh tea. The shape itself isn't new. Ball-shaped compressed tea, called tuan cha (团茶), dates back centuries in Yunnan. The Song Dynasty (960-1279) had its own pressed tea formats. But the single-serving dragon ball as we know it is a contemporary convenience: a way to make pu-erh portable and portion-controlled without sacrificing the benefits of compression.

Most dragon balls weigh between 5 and 8 grams. That's the sweet spot for one gongfu session in a 100-150ml gaiwan or Yixing pot. The leaves are tightly rolled, which means they take longer to unfurl than loose pu-erh. Your first steep will be lighter than usual. By the second or third, the ball has opened up and you're in the full flavor.

You'll find dragon balls made from both sheng (raw) and shou (ripe) pu-erh. Some vendors also roll white tea, dancong oolong, and even aged teas into this format. The principle is the same: pre-portioned, compressed, convenient.

Why compression matters

If you've read our pu-erh tea guide, you know that compression affects aging. Tightly compressed tea ages more slowly than loose leaf because there's less surface area exposed to air and microbes. Dragon balls sit somewhere in the middle. They're compressed, but not as densely as a cake or brick. They age a bit faster than a cake but slower than loose leaf.

For most people, aging isn't the point. Dragon balls are for drinking now. But if you roll a batch and forget about them in a cupboard for a few years, they'll change. Sheng dragon balls will mellow and sweeten. Shou will smooth out further. It's not a bad accident to have.

How to brew pu-erh dragon balls

The main thing to know: dragon balls need patience on the first steep. The compressed shape means the leaves can't release flavor as quickly as loose tea. Don't panic when your first pour comes out pale. The second steep is where it starts.

The rinse

Same as any pu-erh: pour boiling water over the ball, wait about 10 seconds, and discard. This wakes the leaves and starts the unfurling process. With dragon balls, the rinse is doing real work. You're not just washing dust off a cake surface. You're loosening the outer leaves so the interior can breathe.

Some people do a longer rinse for dragon balls, around 20-30 seconds, specifically to help the ball start opening before the first real steep. Worth trying both ways to see what you prefer.

Longer steep compensates for the higher water volume. Still rinse first. The ball won't fully open until midway through. You can re-steep 2-3 times, adding a minute each round.

The key difference from brewing a standard pu-erh cake is timing. With broken-off cake pieces, the leaves are already partially separated and release flavor fast. A dragon ball is a tight sphere that needs to physically open before it gives up much. Plan for that first steep to be a warm-up round. The real session starts at steep two.

If you're new to gongfu brewing, dragon balls are actually a solid way to learn. The portion is pre-measured, so you don't need a scale. Drop a ball in a gaiwan, add water, pour. The format removes the most common beginner mistakes: wrong leaf amount and inconsistent prying.

How to roll your own dragon balls at home

This is the fun part. You don't need a tea factory in Yunnan. You need loose pu-erh, a steamer, a small piece of clean cotton cloth, and about 45 minutes of your time.

What you'll need

  • Loose pu-erh tea (sheng or shou, your choice). Loose maocha (the raw material before compression) is ideal. Loose shou works well too. You can even break apart an inexpensive cake, though the leaves may be more fragmented.
  • A steamer. A bamboo steamer over a pot of boiling water is traditional, but any food steamer works. You just need gentle, consistent steam.
  • Clean cotton cloth. Cheesecloth or a thin cotton handkerchief. About 15cm square per ball. The cloth shapes the ball and absorbs excess moisture.
  • A kitchen scale (optional but helpful). Weigh out 5-8 grams per ball for consistent portions.

The process

1
Portion your leaves

Weigh out 5-8 grams of loose pu-erh per ball. If you don't have a scale, a heaping tablespoon of loose leaf is roughly in the right range. Keep the portions consistent so each ball brews similarly.

2
Steam the leaves

Place a portion of leaves on the steamer rack or in a small bowl set inside the steamer. Steam for 30 to 60 seconds until the leaves are soft and pliable. You want them flexible enough to mold, not cooked. The leaves should darken slightly and feel damp. Be careful here: the steam is hot and the leaves will be too.

3
Wrap and roll

Transfer the steamed leaves onto the center of your cloth square. Gather the corners of the cloth together and twist, forming the leaves into a tight ball inside. Roll the wrapped bundle between your palms, applying gentle pressure to compress the leaves into a sphere. The tighter you roll, the more slowly the ball will open when brewed (and the longer it will hold its shape in storage).

4
Set the shape

Keep the ball wrapped in the cloth and let it sit for about 30 minutes. This lets the compressed shape firm up as the leaves begin to cool and dry. The ball should hold its form when you unwrap it.

5
Dry completely

Unwrap and place the balls on a wire rack or bamboo mat in a well-ventilated area. Let them air-dry completely, which can take 24 to 48 hours depending on humidity. Don't rush this with heat. You want slow, even drying. The balls should feel hard and dry to the touch when they're done.

That's it. Five steps, no special equipment beyond a steamer and some cloth.

Tips from people who've done it

  • Leaf size matters. Larger whole leaves hold together better than broken bits. If your source material is very fragmented, the balls may be crumbly. Maocha with intact leaves gives the best results.
  • Don't over-steam. You want the leaves pliable, not soggy. Soggy leaves take forever to dry and can develop off flavors if they sit damp too long.
  • Experiment with tightness. Looser balls open faster when brewed (shorter first steep). Tighter balls take more patience but may age slightly better if you're storing them.
  • Try it with other teas. White tea, especially aged bai mudan, rolls beautifully into balls. Dancong oolong works too. The dragon ball format isn't exclusive to pu-erh.
  • Label your batches. Write the tea, date, and weight on a small tag. In six months you won't remember which balls are the sheng and which are the shou.

Why bother? The case for dragon balls

If you already have pu-erh cakes and a pu-erh pick, you might wonder what the point is. Fair question. Here's when dragon balls make sense:

  • Travel and office brewing. Toss a few balls in a tin. No pick, no scale, no crumbling cake pieces into a bag. Each one is a self-contained session.
  • Sampling without commitment. Vendors sell individual dragon balls for a few dollars. That's one gongfu session to decide if you like a tea before buying a full cake. Much lower risk than a 357g commitment.
  • Gifting. Dragon balls are visually appealing and self-explanatory. Hand someone a ball and say "put this in hot water." It's a better introduction to pu-erh than handing someone a cake and a sharp metal tool.
  • The rolling process itself. There's something satisfying about handling the warm, steamed leaves and shaping them. It connects you to the tea in a way that breaking off a chunk of cake doesn't. Call it a weekend tea project.

If you're into the idea of tracking different batches (this shou vs that sheng, rolled tight vs loose, aged three months vs fresh), a tea journal helps. Note what you rolled, when, and how it brews compared to the original loose form. You might be surprised how compression changes the character even without long-term aging.

Where to buy dragon balls

Most specialty pu-erh vendors carry them. A few worth looking at:

  • Yunnan Sourcing has a dedicated dragon ball collection spanning sheng, shou, white tea, and black tea formats. Good variety and reasonable prices.
  • White2Tea occasionally includes dragon balls in their monthly tea clubs. Worth watching if you're already subscribed.
  • Seven Cups carries a shou longzhu made from old-growth trees. On the pricier side, but the leaf quality is genuinely high.
  • Teasenz has both raw and ripe options with detailed brewing guides for each.

Prices range widely. Basic shou dragon balls start around $0.50-1.00 per ball. Single-origin sheng from named mountains can run $3-5+ each. Start cheap, figure out what you like, then spend more if the format clicks for you.

Storing dragon balls

Same principles as any pu-erh. Keep them away from strong odors, out of direct sunlight, and in a spot with some airflow. A tin or paper bag in a cupboard works fine for short-term storage (weeks to months).

For longer storage, the same rules from our tea storage guide apply. Dragon balls age a bit faster than full cakes because they have more surface area relative to their mass. If you roll your own, consider making a test batch: brew one fresh, then stash the rest and check back in three months, six months, a year. The comparison is half the fun.
