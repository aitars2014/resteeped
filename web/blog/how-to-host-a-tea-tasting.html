How to Host a Tea Tasting at Home

March 22, 2026 · 8 min read

Wine tastings get all the attention. But a tea tasting is easier to set up, cheaper to run, and nobody needs a designated driver afterward.

Whether you want to introduce friends to loose leaf tea, compare a few teas you've been curious about, or just do something different on a Saturday afternoon, a home tea tasting is one of the best ways to actually learn about tea. Tasting side by side sharpens your palate faster than drinking one tea at a time ever will.

Here's how to set one up from scratch, even if you've never done it before.

Decide on a theme

The biggest mistake people make with tea tastings is trying to cover too much ground. Five random teas from five different categories creates confusion, not clarity. A theme gives your guests a through line.

Some themes that work well:

  • One tea type, multiple origins. Three or four green teas from Japan, China, and Korea. You'll be surprised how different they taste.
  • Light to dark. Start with a white tea, move to green, then oolong, then black. This is the classic tasting progression and it works because each step builds on the last.
  • Single origin, multiple processing. Teas from the same region processed differently. Taiwan is great for this, with everything from light oolongs to heavily roasted ones.
  • Season comparison. Spring harvest vs. autumn harvest of the same tea. Subtle but fascinating differences.
  • Price comparison. A $5 green tea next to a $25 one. Sometimes the expensive tea wins. Sometimes it doesn't. That's the point.

Four to six teas is the sweet spot. Fewer than four and you don't have enough to compare. More than six and palate fatigue sets in.

Choose your teas

If you're working with a theme, the teas mostly choose themselves. But a few practical considerations:

  • Order from lightest to strongest. White and green teas first, oolongs in the middle, black and pu-erh last. This prevents the bold flavors from overwhelming the delicate ones.
  • Buy from independent tea shops. The quality difference between specialty loose leaf and grocery store tea bags is enormous. If you're not sure where to start, Resteeped has over 13,000 teas from 70+ independent shops, all searchable and browsable in one place.
  • Get enough for multiple steepings. Most loose leaf teas can be steeped 3-5 times, and the flavor changes with each infusion. That's part of the fun. Plan for about 3-5 grams per tea per person.

Gather your equipment

You don't need a full gongfu setup (though if you have one, gongfu style is a wonderful way to run a tasting). The basics:

  • A kettle with temperature control. Different teas need different water temperatures. Green tea at boiling will taste bitter. A variable temperature kettle is the single most useful upgrade. Check our steeping temperature guide for the specifics.
  • Small cups. Tasting cups should be small, around 2-3 oz. This keeps people from filling up too fast and lets you focus on the tea itself. White porcelain is ideal because it lets you see the liquor color clearly.
  • One brewing vessel per tea. Small teapots, gaiwans, or even just mugs with infuser baskets. The key is consistency: brew each tea the same way so differences come from the tea, not the method.
  • A timer. Steep times matter. Under-steeped tea tastes thin. Over-steeped tea tastes bitter. A phone timer works fine.
  • Spoons or small plates for dry leaf. Let people see, smell, and touch the dry leaves before brewing. Half the information about a tea comes before water ever touches it.
  • Water. Filtered water, not distilled, not mineral-heavy tap. Water quality affects tea more than most people realize.
  • Paper and pens. Or, better yet, a tea journaling app. More on that in a minute.

Keep it simple

If you're hosting for the first time, don't stress about having the perfect equipment. A kettle, some mugs, and a few good teas are all you actually need. The teas are the star, not the gear.

Set up your tasting station

Arrange your teas in order from lightest to darkest. For each tea, set out:

  1. A small dish of dry leaves
  2. A label with the tea name, type, and origin
  3. The brewing vessel
  4. Enough tasting cups for each guest

If you want to go the extra mile, print a simple tasting sheet with spaces for color, aroma, flavor, and personal notes for each tea. But honestly, keeping a running log on your phone works just as well.

How to taste tea (the actual technique)

Most people drink tea. Tasting tea is a different thing. Walk your guests through this process for each tea:

1. Look at the dry leaves

Notice the shape, size, color, and any visible buds or stems. Tightly rolled oolongs look completely different from flat, needle-shaped green teas. This visual information tells you a lot about how the tea was processed.

2. Smell the dry leaves

Cup the leaves in your hands and breathe in. The dry leaf aroma is often quite different from the brewed tea. You might catch toasted grain, flowers, dried fruit, or honey even before water hits the leaves.

3. Watch the brew

Pour the water and watch the leaves unfurl. The color of the liquor develops in real time, from pale gold to deep amber depending on the tea. This is one of the most meditative parts of the process.

4. Smell the liquor

Before you drink, hold the cup close and inhale. The wet aroma gives you a preview of the flavor. If you're using a gaiwan, smell the lid after pouring. The lid captures volatile aromatics beautifully.

5. Taste

Take a small sip and let it sit on your tongue for a moment before swallowing. Notice where you taste it. Is it on the tip of your tongue, the sides, the back? Does it coat your mouth or disappear quickly? Is there sweetness? Bitterness? Astringency?

The aftertaste, what tea people call "huigan," is worth paying attention to. Some teas finish sweet even when the initial sip is savory or vegetal. A long, pleasant aftertaste is one of the hallmarks of quality.

6. Re-steep and compare

Most quality loose leaf teas can be steeped multiple times, and each infusion reveals something different. The second steeping is often the best. Do at least two rounds of each tea so your guests can see how the flavor evolves.

Taking notes (the part most people skip)

Tasting without notes is like studying without writing anything down. You'll enjoy it in the moment and forget most of it by tomorrow.

Encourage your guests to jot down what they notice for each tea. It doesn't need to be formal. "Tastes like autumn" is a perfectly valid tasting note. The goal is to build a personal reference point, not write a sommelier's report.

If you want a more structured approach, note these for each tea:

  • Appearance: Color of the liquor (straw, gold, amber, copper, mahogany)
  • Aroma: What does it smell like? (floral, grassy, roasted, fruity, earthy)
  • Flavor: What does it taste like? Where do you taste it?
  • Body: Thin and light, or thick and full?
  • Finish: Does the flavor linger? Does it change after you swallow?
  • Rating: Would you buy this tea again?

A tea journal is the easiest way to keep all of this organized over time. If you're tasting regularly, your notes from six months ago become genuinely useful when deciding what to re-order or recommend to friends.

Food pairings (optional but fun)

Keep food simple and neutral so it doesn't compete with the tea. Good options:

  • Plain crackers or rice cakes to cleanse the palate between teas
  • Lightly sweetened mochi pairs well with Japanese green teas
  • Dark chocolate is surprisingly good with roasted oolongs and pu-erh
  • Fresh fruit works with lighter teas, especially white and green
  • Cheese is unconventional but aged gouda with a roasted oolong is a real thing

Avoid anything strongly flavored, spicy, or heavily seasoned. The tea should be the focus.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Using boiling water for every tea. This is the fastest way to ruin a delicate green or white tea. Match your water temperature to the tea type.
  • Rushing through teas. Give each tea at least 10-15 minutes. People need time to taste, think, and discuss. Slow is the whole point.
  • Too many teas. Six is plenty. Eight is pushing it. Twelve is a recipe for palate fatigue and a lot of leftover tea.
  • Skipping the re-steep. Multiple infusions are where tea gets really interesting. Don't move on after one round.
  • No context. Tell your guests a little about each tea, where it's from, how it's processed, what makes it interesting. A sentence or two of context changes the entire tasting.

After the tasting

The best part of a tea tasting is the conversation it creates. People discover they have strong preferences they didn't know about. Someone who thinks they don't like green tea tries a roasted hojicha and has a revelation. Someone else discovers that the cheapest tea in the lineup was their favorite.

If your guests want to remember what they tried (and most will), point them toward Resteeped. Every tea they tasted can be logged with their personal notes, so they can find it again later when they're ordering for themselves. It's a much better system than scribbling on a napkin.

The short version

  1. Pick a theme (one tea type across origins, light-to-dark progression, or price comparison)
  2. Choose 4-6 teas, ordered lightest to darkest
  3. Get a temperature-controlled kettle, small cups, and one brewer per tea
  4. For each tea: look at the leaves, smell dry, brew, smell wet, taste, note, re-steep
  5. Take notes. Seriously. You'll forget otherwise.
  6. Keep food simple and neutral
  7. Don't rush. Give each tea room to breathe.

That's it. No certification required, no special training, no expensive equipment. Just good tea, good company, and the willingness to pay attention to what's in your cup.
