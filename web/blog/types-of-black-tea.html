Types of Black Tea: A Complete Guide to Every Style Worth Knowing

March 19, 2026 · 12 min read

Black tea accounts for roughly 80% of all tea consumed in the Western world. But "black tea" is about as specific as "red wine." It tells you the general category and almost nothing about what's actually in your cup.

A malty, thick Assam brewed strong with milk and a delicate, floral first-flush Darjeeling are both black teas. So is a pine-smoked Lapsang Souchong that smells like a campfire. The range is enormous, and once you start exploring it, grocery-store tea bags start feeling like a very small window into a very large world.

This guide covers every major type of black tea worth knowing: where it comes from, what it tastes like, how to brew it, and what makes each one distinct. Whether you're just moving beyond English Breakfast or you're trying to make sense of Chinese hong cha, this is your map.

A quick note on terminology

In the West, we call it "black tea." In China, the same category is called hong cha, which translates to "red tea," a reference to the coppery-red color of the liquor, not the leaves. Both terms refer to fully oxidized tea from the Camellia sinensis plant. If you see "red tea" on a Chinese tea menu, that's black tea. (Rooibos is also sometimes called "red tea," but it's a completely different plant from South Africa. We covered that distinction in our herbal tea vs real tea guide.)

All black tea is fully oxidized, meaning the leaves are rolled and exposed to air until they darken completely before being fired to stop the process. This is what separates black tea from oolong (partially oxidized) and green tea (minimally oxidized). The full oxidation is what gives black tea its characteristic bold flavor, darker color, and generally higher caffeine content.

Indian black teas

India is the world's second-largest tea producer and home to two of the most recognizable names in tea.

Assam

Assam comes from the Brahmaputra Valley in northeastern India, a hot, humid lowland that produces teas with incredible body and malt character. The cultivar grown here, Camellia sinensis var. assamica, has larger leaves than the Chinese variety and produces a bolder, more robust cup.

Flavor profile: Malty, rich, full-bodied. Notes of bread crust, brown sugar, sometimes a hint of chocolate. Strong enough to stand up to milk and sugar without disappearing.

Two main styles:

  • CTC (crush-tear-curl): The leaves are machine-processed into small, uniform pellets. This is what fills most tea bags and commercial blends like English Breakfast and Irish Breakfast. Brews fast, dark, and strong. Designed for efficiency, not subtlety.
  • Orthodox: Whole-leaf Assam processed by hand or semi-automated methods. More complex, less astringent, and worth seeking out if you only know Assam from tea bags. Second-flush Assam (harvested June-July) is considered the best, with the most concentrated malty sweetness.

Brewing: 200-212°F (93-100°C), 3-5 minutes. CTC can go shorter (2-3 minutes) since the small particles extract quickly. Orthodox whole-leaf benefits from a full 4-5 minutes.

Assam lands at the higher end of tea caffeine content, typically 50-90 mg per cup depending on processing and brew time.

Darjeeling

Darjeeling is grown in the foothills of the Himalayas in West Bengal, at elevations between 2,000 and 7,000 feet. The altitude, cool temperatures, and misty conditions produce a tea that's dramatically different from Assam, despite both being Indian black teas.

Flavor profile: Light, muscatel, floral. Often described as having a "champagne" quality. The best Darjeelings have a natural grape-like sweetness and a clean, bright finish that lingers. This is not a tea that wants milk.

The flushes matter here:

  • First flush (March-April): Light, green-gold liquor, floral and delicate. Some first-flush Darjeelings are barely oxidized and look more like an oolong than a black tea. These are the most sought-after and most expensive.
  • Second flush (May-June): Deeper amber color, the classic muscatel character. Fuller body than first flush but still lighter than most other black teas. This is peak Darjeeling for most drinkers.
  • Autumn flush (October-November): Darker, rounder, less complex. Good value, solid daily drinker, but without the high notes of the earlier flushes.

Brewing: 195-205°F (90-96°C), 3-4 minutes. First flush especially benefits from slightly lower temperatures to preserve its delicacy. Boiling water will flatten a good Darjeeling.

Nilgiri

Nilgiri comes from the Blue Mountains of southern India, and it's the overlooked third member of India's tea regions. Nilgiri teas are generally clean, bright, and brisk, without the malt of Assam or the complexity of Darjeeling. They're the workhorse behind many commercial blends.

Flavor profile: Clean, brisk, slightly fruity. Medium body. Less character than Assam or Darjeeling, but reliable and versatile. Makes an excellent iced tea base.

Brewing: 200-212°F, 3-5 minutes. Forgiving and hard to over-brew.

Chinese black teas (hong cha)

China is where tea began, and Chinese black teas tend to be smoother, more nuanced, and less aggressive than their Indian counterparts. Where Indian blacks punch, Chinese blacks glide.

Keemun (Qimen)

Keemun comes from Qimen County in Anhui province and is considered one of China's most distinguished black teas. It was the original Chinese tea exported to Europe and the traditional base of English Breakfast blends before cheaper Indian teas replaced it.

Flavor profile: Smooth, slightly smoky, with notes of dried fruit, cocoa, and a wine-like depth. Lower astringency than Indian blacks. Some high-grade Keemuns have an orchid-like fragrance.

Brewing: 195-205°F (90-96°C), 3-5 minutes. Can also be brewed gongfu-style with good results. See our gongfu brewing guide for the method.

Yunnan Gold (Dian Hong)

Dian Hong comes from Yunnan province in southwestern China, the birthplace of all tea. The best Yunnan Golds are made from pure buds (golden tips), giving the dry leaf a striking golden-orange appearance.

Flavor profile: Sweet, honeyed, with notes of roasted sweet potato, malt, and sometimes cocoa or pepper. Much smoother and sweeter than Indian blacks. Very low bitterness. The golden-tip versions are exceptionally smooth.

Brewing: 195-205°F (90-96°C), 3-4 minutes. These teas respond beautifully to gongfu brewing, unfolding across 6-8 steeps with shifting flavors.

Lapsang Souchong (Zheng Shan Xiao Zhong)

Lapsang Souchong from the Wuyi Mountains in Fujian is considered the world's first black tea. The traditional version is smoke-dried over pinewood fires, giving it that unmistakable campfire aroma. It's polarizing. You either love it or you're startled by it.

Flavor profile: Pine smoke, dried longan, leather. Underneath the smoke, there's a natural sweetness and fruity depth. The highest-grade versions (Zheng Shan Xiao Zhong from Tongmu village) are barely smoked or unsmoked, with a deep sweetness that surprises people who only know the heavily smoked commercial versions.

Brewing: 200-212°F (93-100°C), 3-5 minutes. Stands up to boiling water. The smoked versions are excellent for cold weather, cooking (smoked tea-rubbed meats), and cocktails.

Jin Jun Mei

Jin Jun Mei is a relatively new style from the same Wuyi Mountain region as Lapsang Souchong, created in 2005. Made entirely from tiny spring buds, it takes roughly 80,000 buds to produce one kilogram of finished tea. This makes it one of the most labor-intensive (and expensive) teas in the world.

Flavor profile: Sweet potato, honey, dried lychee, caramel. No smoke at all. Incredibly smooth with a lasting sweetness in the throat (what the Chinese call hui gan). This is a black tea that can convert green tea drinkers.

Brewing: 185-195°F (85-90°C), 2-3 minutes. Lower temperature than most blacks to protect the delicate buds. Excellent for gongfu brewing.

Sri Lankan black teas (Ceylon)

Sri Lanka (formerly Ceylon) produces tea at varying elevations, and the altitude is the primary driver of flavor.

  • High-grown (4,000+ feet): Light, bright, citrusy, with a crisp finish. The best high-grown Ceylons have a champagne-like quality similar to Darjeeling. Nuwara Eliya and Dimbula are the key regions.
  • Mid-grown (2,000-4,000 feet): Medium body, balanced. Clean and approachable.
  • Low-grown (below 2,000 feet): Full-bodied, malty, deep color. Closer in character to Assam. Good for milk tea.

Brewing: 200-212°F, 3-5 minutes. Ceylon teas are among the most forgiving to brew and make excellent iced teas. If you're exploring cold brewing, Ceylon is a reliable starting point.

African black teas

Kenyan

Kenya is the world's third-largest tea producer and the biggest exporter by volume. Most Kenyan tea is CTC-processed for the commercial market, but the country is increasingly producing high-quality orthodox teas worth paying attention to.

Flavor profile: Bold, brisk, slightly citrusy. Kenyan black teas have a distinctive brightness and punch. The orthodox versions can be surprisingly floral.

Rwandan and Malawian

Smaller East African producers making increasingly interesting teas. Rwandan blacks tend to be clean and bright with berry notes. Malawian teas are rich and malty. These are worth exploring if you want to move beyond the usual origins.

Blended and flavored black teas

Many of the black teas people know best are blends, not single-origin teas.

  • English Breakfast: Traditionally a blend of Assam, Ceylon, and Kenyan teas. Designed to be bold enough for milk. Every brand's blend is different.
  • Irish Breakfast: Heavier on Assam, stronger and maltier than English Breakfast. Built for milk and sugar.
  • Earl Grey: Black tea (usually Ceylon or a blend) scented with bergamot oil, a citrus fruit from Italy. The bergamot adds a distinctive floral-citrus note. Quality varies wildly. Good Earl Grey uses real bergamot oil, not synthetic flavoring.
  • Chai: Black tea (Assam or CTC) brewed with spices (cardamom, cinnamon, ginger, cloves, black pepper) and often milk. We have a full chai recipe if you want to make it from scratch.

How to brew black tea (general rules)

Black tea is the most forgiving category to brew, but there are still differences worth noting.

  • CTC and broken-leaf teas: 200-212°F, 2-3 minutes. These extract fast. Going longer makes them harsh.
  • Whole-leaf Indian blacks: 200-212°F, 3-5 minutes. They need time to unfurl and release flavor.
  • Chinese blacks (Keemun, Dian Hong, Jin Jun Mei): 190-205°F, 3-4 minutes. Slightly lower temperature preserves their smoothness. These also shine with gongfu brewing.
  • Delicate blacks (first-flush Darjeeling, Jin Jun Mei): 185-200°F, 2-4 minutes. Treat these more like an oolong than a breakfast tea.

How to start exploring

If you've been drinking tea-bag black tea and want to explore further, here's a practical path:

  1. Start with orthodox Assam. If you already like strong breakfast teas, whole-leaf Assam will feel familiar but noticeably richer and more complex. It's the easiest upgrade.
  2. Try a Yunnan Gold. This is the bridge tea. Sweet, smooth, zero bitterness. If you've ever thought "I don't really like black tea," Yunnan Gold might change your mind.
  3. Explore a second-flush Darjeeling. This will show you how light and nuanced black tea can be. Drink it plain. No milk, no sugar.
  4. Go deep on Chinese blacks. Keemun, Jin Jun Mei, Lapsang Souchong (both smoked and unsmoked). This is where black tea gets genuinely interesting.
